Visual Teaching Philosophy

A hand‑made book of beliefs.

This Visual Teaching Philosophy is a hand‑made book — bound, painted, printed, and stitched over the course of a semester. Each page below holds one part of what I have come to believe about teaching. Together, they form a portrait of the kind of educator I am becoming: one who trusts curiosity, honors nature, and treats every student as a whole person.

Teaching Philosophy Statement

Teaching as a Walk Under the Tree

When I picture my classroom, I do not always picture a classroom. Sometimes I picture a tree on a wide lawn. Children gather around it — some climbing, some sitting in its shade, some pointing at a flower they have just noticed. I am there too, not as the source of the tree, but as someone walking alongside them, listening to what they see. This image, which I painted in my sketchbook early in the semester, has become the foundation of my teaching philosophy. Teaching, for me, is not about transmitting information inside four walls. It is about helping students feel, imagine, and make meaning together — wherever that meaning is most alive.

This belief is deeply shaped by Rousseau’s vision of natural education. I read Rousseau in two ways at once. First, “natural” means following what is alive in a child — their questions, their wonder, their need to touch and try. Second, “natural” means the actual world outside the classroom: the lawn, the trees, the wind, the sound of rain on bamboo. Learning does not have to happen inside a room with a roof. A leaf can teach symmetry better than a worksheet. A walk can teach observation better than a lecture. My role is to be what Rousseau called the “invisible guide”: present, attentive, but never replacing the student’s own discovery with my voice. Beatty, Leigh, and Lund Dean (2009) remind teachers that our classroom practices are inseparable from our underlying beliefs about knowledge and human nature, and the belief I keep returning to is that children are not empty containers to be filled. They are whole people with experiences, feelings, and different ways of understanding the world.

This is why I treat the classroom — indoor or outdoor — as a space of dialogue rather than delivery. Pratt (2005) cautions that teaching philosophies can become hollow slogans if they do not honestly reveal what is essential to a teacher’s practice; what is essential to mine is the conviction that meaning is co‑constructed. When I plan a lesson, I ask not “what will I tell them?” but “what will we notice together?” In a recent math lesson on symmetry, my co‑teachers and I taped a line down the classroom floor and let students become the line of symmetry with their own bodies, mirroring each other to Bach. The geometry was not handed to them. It rose out of their movement, their laughter, their corrections of one another. I imagine taking the same lesson outside next time — finding symmetry in leaves, in butterfly wings, in the shadows of a building. The everyday world is already full of curriculum; we only need to help students see it.

Building these moments depends on relationships. I develop and maintain positive relationships with students by beginning with their names, their stories, and the languages they bring. Kim (2021) writes powerfully about how the simple act of pronouncing a student’s name correctly is a form of antiracist, humanizing pedagogy — a way of saying, you belong here as you are. I want my classroom to be a place where a child’s Mandarin name, their Vietnamese tones, their Spanish syllables are not asked to disappear at the door. Supporting cultural, racial, gender, intellectual, and language diversity means more than tolerance; it means building lessons in which different ways of knowing — visual, embodied, linguistic, musical, environmental — are all treated as legitimate paths into understanding.

This is also where the arts enter, not as decoration but as thinking. Eisner (2002) argues that the arts teach us that there are many ways to know the world, and that these ways belong inside education, not beside it. When students paint, fold, move, or build, they are not stepping away from rigorous learning — they are doing it. Sometimes a child cannot yet say what they understand, but they can show it in color, shape, or gesture. My job is to make room for those forms of knowing, to step outside when the air is good, and to walk under the tree with them.

Teaching, in the end, is relational, imaginative, and shared. It is a practice of staying curious alongside the curious.
